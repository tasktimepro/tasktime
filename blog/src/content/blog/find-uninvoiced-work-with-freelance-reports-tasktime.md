Missed billable work is one of the quietest ways freelancers lose money.

It usually is not dramatic. You track a small task, forget to add it to an invoice, leave a billable expense unbilled, or finish a project without reviewing the final time entries. One item is not much. Over a year, it adds up.

TaskTime reports help catch that work before it disappears into memory.

## What counts as uninvoiced work?

Uninvoiced work is billable work that exists in your records but has not been billed yet.

That can include:

- Billable time entries
- Billable task work
- Billable expenses
- Project work that has been tracked but not attached to an invoice

If the work is not visible before invoice day, it is easy to miss.

For searches like "unbilled time tracker" or "uninvoiced work report," this is the practical need: find what should be billed before the invoice goes out.

## Time tracking is only useful if it reaches the invoice

Tracking time is good. Invoicing the right time is better.

A timer can tell you what happened, but it does not help much if the records stay disconnected from billing. TaskTime connects time entries to tasks, tasks to projects, projects to clients, and invoices to the work they bill for.

That connection is what makes reports useful.

When you review billable hours, you are not just looking at a timesheet. You are looking at work that can become an invoice.

## Use the Hours Worked report

The Hours Worked report helps you review logged time by project and client.

It can show:

- Total hours
- Billable hours
- Unbilled billable hours
- Entry counts
- Project and client assignment

That helps you spot gaps before invoicing. If a project has billable hours but no recent invoice, it deserves a closer look.

It also helps with non-hourly work. Even on flat-rate projects, tracked time shows whether the project stayed healthy or quietly took more effort than expected.

## Watch billable utilization

Billable utilization is the percentage of tracked time that was billable.

For freelancers, this is a useful business signal. A week full of admin, sales, edits, and unpaid support can feel busy, but billable utilization shows how much of that time actually moved paid work forward.

TaskTime reports include billable utilization so you can compare:

- Total hours worked
- Billable hours worked
- Non-billable overhead

The goal is not to make every hour billable. Freelancing always has admin. The goal is to see the pattern clearly enough to adjust.

## Review billable expenses too

Uninvoiced work is not only time.

Billable expenses can also be missed:

- Hosting costs
- Stock assets
- Travel
- Materials
- Client-specific software
- Contractor costs

If an expense should be passed through to a client, it needs to be visible before the invoice is finalized.

TaskTime reports can include uninvoiced expense totals, and invoice generation can pull billable expenses into the invoice workflow. That reduces the chance that reimbursable costs stay buried in your expense list.

## Use project work summaries for client clarity

Sometimes you need more than a total.

A project work summary helps explain what was done during a period. That can be useful when a client asks why an invoice includes a certain number of hours, or when you want to share a concise activity report with a monthly invoice.

Because the work summary is based on tracked tasks and time, it is more reliable than writing a recap from memory.

This is especially useful for retainers and long-running projects where the client wants visibility without reading every task note.

## A simple pre-invoice checklist

Before sending invoices, run this quick review:

1. Open Reports.
2. Choose the billing period.
3. Review Hours Worked.
4. Check unbilled billable hours.
5. Check uninvoiced expenses.
6. Review project work summaries for active clients.
7. Generate invoices from the work that should be billed.

That small routine can prevent a lot of missed revenue.
