Freelancers do not usually get paid late because they forgot how much they charged.

They get paid late because follow-up becomes messy. One invoice is open, another is partially discussed in email, an older one was paid, and the client asks for a summary before approving payment.

That is where outstanding invoice reports and client statements help.

## What an outstanding invoice report answers

An outstanding invoice report focuses on unpaid invoices.

In TaskTime, it helps answer:

- Which invoices are still open?
- Which invoices are overdue?
- How much is outstanding?
- How many days overdue is each invoice?
- Which client or project does each invoice belong to?
- What is the due date?
- What currency is the invoice in?

That gives you a follow-up list without opening every invoice individually.

If you search for "unpaid invoice tracker" or "outstanding invoice report," this is the core need: a reliable view of who owes what.

## Why overdue visibility matters

Late payment is easier to handle early.

If you only notice unpaid invoices when cash gets tight, the follow-up is already late. A weekly or monthly outstanding invoice review keeps the list visible.

TaskTime separates outstanding and overdue invoices in report summaries, so you can see both:

- Outstanding: invoices still open
- Overdue: open invoices past their due date

That distinction matters. An invoice can be open but not late yet. Overdue invoices need a different kind of follow-up.

## Use aging to prioritize follow-up

An invoice that is two days overdue is different from one that is forty-five days overdue.

Invoice aging helps you prioritize. Instead of treating every unpaid invoice the same, you can focus on the older balances first.

TaskTime's outstanding report includes overdue context, due dates, and days overdue so you can decide what needs a friendly reminder, what needs a firmer note, and what may need a direct conversation.

## Client statements are the bigger picture

Sometimes a client does not need one invoice. They need the account history.

A client statement summarizes activity for a selected client, including opening balance, invoices, payments, and closing balance for the chosen period.

That is useful when:

- A client has multiple invoices
- Payments cover more than one invoice
- You need to show what changed during a period
- A finance team asks for a statement
- You want a cleaner follow-up than a long email thread

For freelancers, a client statement can make you look much more organized without adding much admin.

## Filter before exporting

Reports are more useful when the filter matches the question.

In TaskTime, you can filter reports by:

- Period
- Business profile
- Client
- Project
- Invoice status
- Income date basis
- Currency display mode

That means you can create a focused view before exporting. For example:

- All unpaid invoices for one client
- Overdue invoices for one business profile
- A client statement for last quarter
- Source-currency balances for an international client
- Preferred-currency totals for cash-flow review

The filter step keeps exports smaller and more useful.

## Keep source currencies visible

If you invoice international clients, outstanding balances may exist in multiple currencies.

TaskTime can show report totals in your preferred currency when exchange rates are available, or show source currencies when you want the original invoice amounts.

That matters for follow-up. A client should see the amount in the currency you invoiced them. Your own cash-flow overview may be easier in your preferred currency.

Both views are useful for different reasons.

## Export the report when needed

Sometimes the report is just for you. Sometimes you need a file.

TaskTime can export report data so you can share it, archive it, or send it to your accountant.

For outstanding invoices, that gives you structured records with invoice number, client, business, project, invoice date, due date, status, days overdue, currency, and total.

That is much cleaner than a screenshot or a manually typed email list.

## A practical follow-up routine

Try this once a week:

1. Open Reports.
2. Go to Outstanding.
3. Filter to non-draft unpaid invoices.
4. Sort or scan for the oldest overdue items.
5. Open the relevant invoice.
6. Send a reminder if needed.
7. Export a client statement when the client needs more context.

This keeps payment follow-up calm and factual.

## Why this helps cash flow

Cash flow is not only about earning more. It is about seeing payment delays early enough to act.

Outstanding invoice reports make unpaid work visible. Client statements make client account history easier to explain. Together, they reduce the time you spend reconstructing what happened.

That is the real benefit for freelancers: fewer payment surprises, clearer follow-up, and less invoice admin.
